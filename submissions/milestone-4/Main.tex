\section{Part A: Experiment Execution}

\subsection{Step 1: Pilot Testing} 
\par
\indent
We conducted a pilot study with two subjects to validate our experimental conditions, identify potential bottlenecks, and refine our methodology prior to the main experiment. The pilot revealed several areas requiring adjustment, primarily regarding task appropriateness and instruction clarity.

First, subjects initially found the Budgeting task instructions ambiguous, which caused confusion, increased completion times, and exaggerated the learning effect. This was because they completed the second interface much faster once they finally understood the goal. We reworded the instructions to be more straightforward and added a concrete example of the expected output to avoid confusion on what they must do. We also found the initial task too simple to represent realistic, slightly more complicated scenarios. We modified the task to better reflect real-world complexity. Next, the spreadsheet template contained complex, irrelevant elements that distracted subjects. We removed these extraneous components to focus strictly on the scope of the tasks. 

Finally, we decided that we would exclude completion time as a dependent variable for budgeting. Time was heavily influenced by the users' prior spreadsheet familiarity and variations in task interpretation, introducing unwanted variability into our data. Since our focus is on interface usability and user experience, we deprioritized time as a metric to focus on measures that more directly reflect these aspects. However, future iterations with more controlled conditions can incorporate time-based analysis. Most of the deatils on the changes were covered on the experimental description section.

Fortunately, other aspects of the protocol performed exactly as intended. The overall session length averaged 50 minutes, which aligned with our expectations. Furthermore, our study instruments, specifically the post-task questionnaires and the NASA-TLX scale proved highly useful. They effectively captured clear, qualitative feedback and opinions regarding the subjects' experiences with the interfaces.

\section{Part B: Final Project Conclusions, Recommendations, and Reflection}
\subsection{Step 7: Final Conclusions and Recommendations}
Reflecting on our design, we can conclude that both our budgeting and ideation interface require many adjustments, but our overall design being validated. First, for budgeting, our overall design that focused on the automated auto-split, along with the check boxes to select members was liked by almost all participants. One design flaw encountered within budgeting was that editing expense distributions after creating the expense was not possible; some participants made the mistake of inputting the wrong value and had to recreate the expense from scratch in order to finish the task. Several noted they had “no way to edit” or “couldn’t go back,” which affected their experience. 
\newline
\\ \indent
For ideation, many participants found the swiping feature enjoyable and novel. Furthermore, they found the organized list of ideas, along with the filters, easy to understand and parse information from. They described the app as having everything “in one place,” while group chat required them to “scroll and keep track.” One major adjustment that our design can incorporate is adding some sort of discussion forum or comment section for each idea; many participants pointed out that group chats allow constant discussions between members, which our application alone does not provide. 
\newline
\\ \indent More details are covered on our experiment report; specifically, the "Interpretation of Results", "Design Implications", and "Conclusion".

\vspace{10pt}

\subsection{Step 8: Design and Evaluation Process Reflection}

User participation helped clarify our goals and adjust our design direction. The interviews conducted in Milestone 2 taught us that tools such as spreadsheets and group chats are often used in the process of planning and managing group travel information. In addition, they clearly identified pet peeves and common work required in the travel process. For example, common problems that were introduced were using spreadsheets to manually manage and maintain expenses from the trip, and the lack of engagement when coming up with ideas and making final decisions. By prioritizing key issues that actual users face, we were able to us to narrow our scope down to enhancing user experience for budgeting and ideation processes. 
\newline

Being able to do a walkthrough using our low fidelity prototype assisted in finding any potential bottlenecks and make necessary changes prior to the experiment with actual users. This step helped us to put ourselves into the target users' shoes and find bottlenecks of our application. Through this process, we were able to modify and verify our initial design, focusing more on user experience rather than just the solution to our aimed problems. Our updated changes were then applied for our medium fidelity prototype which was used in our experiments. 
\newline 

Overall, outside of some of the issues we listed in our report, we would say the methods chosen for our evaluation helped us achieve what we were looking for. Choosing budgeting and ideation made sense from a user requirements sense since they were common issues talked about, and they were also the most useful topics in an experiment since they were more interactive and would thus yield more interesting results. Focusing on qualitative data also gave us higher-quality data that we could carry into improving our interface. That being said, the things we learned from our users were helpful but were often not entirely surprising. For example, we knew that it would be hard for users to switch to another app, as humans are creatures of habit.

\subsubsection{Ideation Scenarios}
\paragraph{(Telegram Poll): Planning a Trip to Seoul}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scenario:} Imagine a scenario where you and your four friends are planning a trip to Seoul. You are currently in a Telegram group chat and are deciding on activities and ideas for the trip.
    \item \textbf{Setup:} The Telegram group chat consists of 10 separate ``polls'' that represent different activities/ideas. The chat also includes discussion and messages from your group members about these activities as well.
    \item \textbf{Goal:} Your goal is to navigate through the chat and cast your own, genuine personal vote (``Yes'' or ``No/Skip'') for all 10 activities/ideas. Once you have finished voting, let us know.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(Ideation Application Interface): Planning a Trip to Tokyo}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scenario:} Imagine a scenario where you and your four friends are planning a trip to Tokyo. You are currently using a dedicated travel application to decide on activities and ideas for the trip.
    \item \textbf{Setup:} The application's ideation feature consists of 10 separate ``idea cards'' for different activities/ideas. Each card includes a brief description from your group members about the activity.
    \item \textbf{Goal:} Your goal is to navigate through the interface and cast your own, genuine personal vote (``Like'' or ``Skip'') for all 10 activities/ideas. Once you have finished voting, let us know.
\end{itemize}
